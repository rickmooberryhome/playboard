The recruiting problem

Players want to play college football. Most do not know the next move.

Recruiting gets messy fast. Film, Hudl, X, coach emails, school fit, camps, grades, and follow-ups all matter. Without a plan, the athlete ends up guessing.

He does not know who to contact.

A dream-school list is not enough. He needs realistic-fit schools and the right coach contacts.

He does not know what to send.

Film, Hudl, X, grades, and the first email all need to work together.

He does not know when to follow up.

Most recruiting momentum dies because there is no board, no tracking, and no next step.

Where athletes get stuck

He does not need more random recruiting advice. He needs the next right step.

The hard part is not wanting to play college football. The hard part is knowing what matters right now: which schools fit, what film to show, what to say to coaches, and how to keep moving when nobody responds yet.

Frequently asked questions

Questions families usually ask first.

Is this only for athletes who already have college interest?

No. PlayBoard can help athletes who already have interest and athletes who are just getting started. The plan changes based on where the athlete stands right now.

Do parents or athletes communicate with PlayBoard?

Both. Parents handle consent, payment, and receive weekly updates. Athletes receive weekly recruiting guidance and accountability.

Will PlayBoard contact college coaches for my athlete?

No. We help prepare the athlete, build the plan, improve the message, and track follow-ups. The athlete should be the one communicating with coaches.

What if my athlete does not know his GPA, test score, or target schools?

That is normal. PlayBoard explains why the information matters and gives him a clear next step to find it.

How much time does this take each week?

Most athletes should expect 1 to 5+ hours per week depending on the time of year, how much the athlete commits, and how fast he needs to move.

Why include parent updates?

Parents should know what is happening, but they should not have to run the whole process. Updates keep parents informed while the athlete owns the work.
